My graduate-level research at Teachers College, Columbia University, has focused on evaluating cognitive development in school-based makerspace programs. Key areas of interest are systems reasoning, creativity, human-technology interaction, mathematical development, and metacognition. I have also designed activities to develop early systems reasoning skills using both traditional and digital tools, engaging students in math talk, causal explanations, and perspective-taking. About the Research

Makerspaces or invention labs are appearing in schools, after-school programs, libraries, and museums. They are spaces for learning, creativity, and collaboration. As schools begin to experiment with maker programs, leaders must decide how much money to invest and how to distribute resources among spaces, tools, and facilitators. Schools can take the first step by initiating a culture of making.

But how do leaders evaluate whether a maker program has established a culture of making with unique learning opportunities, and are there observable differences between the use of digital versus traditional materials?

I explore roles, role exchanges, and role development in participants making and inventing complex systems to assess perspective-taking, systems reasoning, and creative action. Profiles of participants, projects, and schools are based on ethnographic research. Data visualizations model systems of roles and creativity.
